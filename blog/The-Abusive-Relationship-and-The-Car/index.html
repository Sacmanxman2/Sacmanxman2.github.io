The Abusive Relationship and the Car

November 10, 2017

Reading time ~2 minutes

God is amazing. God is perfect. Somehow he loves us when we’re definitely, definitely not.

I fell pretty hard into an old sin habit the other day, as we’re all prone to do. Of course, once I fully realized what I had done I felt terrible, since it’s one of those sin habits that just doesn’t want to go away. I was praying to God asking for forgiveness, etc. when the full extent of his grace dawned on me. I remembered two things:

First of all, it’s a relationship, but since one of us is perfect and the other is far from it, it’s really an abusive relationship. We’re the abuser. Think about that for a sec. It’s an abusive relationship in which we are the abuser.

Why does God continue to stay in this abusive relationship? It’s certainly not good for us to stay in those kinds of relationships. I think it’s because of how perfect and good God is. He can’t be dragged down by us. We add nothing to him, we take nothing from him. His grace just overflows into us. Through Jesus’ death on the cross, we’re saved from the penalty to our sin and somehow God is able to not only tolerate us, but love us and enjoy us through it!

God is also our father, and he gives great gifts as he says. There’s an analogy I once heard somewhere that I will expound apon slightly, which is the other thing I remembered after I fell again:

Imagine a father who, being incredibly wealthy, gives his son a sports car. The son loves the car, and loves to drive it, but sometimes he get’s a bit rowdy. Perhaps he’s just really had a rough day, or he’s feeling that teenage angst that we’ve all felt at one point. He drives a bit too fast, takes a turn a bit too fast, and crashes into a pole. He gets the car fixed, nobody is hurt, but it’s still pretty rough. One would think that he would learn from this and drive carefully, but instead, when his dad purchases a new car, he does it again, except this time going even faster. He thinks to himself “I can control it this time! I’m a better driver than I was then!”. Of course, he’s lying to himself. While he may be a better driver, he’s really just intoxicated with the adrenaline, the anger that he feels, the pain, whatever’s going on, that he crashes again, this time almost hurting someone in the process.

Yet his dad fixes the car for him.

This happens several more times, each time increasing in damage. He totals the car several times, but his dad gives him new ones each time. His dad is fully aware of how his son will handle the car, but believes that eventually he will learn.

The analogy starts to break down here (haha puns), but I think it can be useful to think about God this way. He’s given us something, which we continue to abuse and hurt ourselves and sometimes others with, and while we have to live with the consequences of our actions, he is graceful and merciful in how he repairs the damage we caused, and helps us grow along the way.
